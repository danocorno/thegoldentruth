Hints of Truth

Posted by Nancy

I love to see people's reactions when they first hear about The Golden Truth. I'm a student filmmaker working on a feature length with my best friend, so naturally people assume that my movie is about drugs, sex, violence, or a combination of these. While I am flattered that people think so highly of my cinematic taste level, no one has guessed that my film was about pediatric cancer. Although my age and background adds to their surprise, I contribute most people's initial shock to their lack of understanding when it comes to pediatric oncology.
I am actually shocked by how many people don't know that childhood cancer exists. There is a large population of people who think cancer is "only for old people" or is simply the result of excessive smoking, drinking, etc. But once you get past the people who say "kids get cancer!?", you get the people who say "awh, I bet you spend a lot of time at Disney!" or "isn't that depressing?!" That brings me to the entire mission of The Golden Truth: changing the way we see childhood cancer.
There are two main misconceptions about pediatric cancer:
1. Pediatric cancer is full of happy, bald children who go to Disney World and meet celebrities for free.
2. Pediatric cancer is depressing, and we should avoid thinking about it.
Whether people don't see the problem, or ignore the problem altogether, there is a serious lack of awareness in the childhood cancer community. The little you see about childhood cancer in the media is often sugarcoated enough to make you forget the monster these kids are facing. That's why I'm striving to provide people with the truth. I realize that captivating an audience is a tough thing to do, especially when that audience has already made a point to ignore and sugarcoat my topic of choice. But I believe that with the right approach, we can not only change the way people view the pediatric oncology field, but the way we treat their patients altogether.
Even people who understand the challenges that childhood cancer patients are facing question the ability of a film to create change. But in our digital age, the media is the most powerful tool available to us. I wholeheartedly believe that if we do this right, people will have no choice but to take action. By combining powerful images of truth, and providing the hope for a cure: we CAN change pediatric oncology for the better. Will you join us?
